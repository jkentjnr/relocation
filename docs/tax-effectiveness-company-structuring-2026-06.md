## 1. James's Baseline Malta Personal Tax Position

> **TLDR:** As a non-Maltese-domiciliary resident, James pays Malta tax on (1) Malta-source income and (2) foreign-source income remitted to Malta. Foreign income kept offshore is untaxed in Malta — but a minimum tax of ~€5,000 per married couple per year applies when foreign-source income exceeds €35,000 and is not fully brought in. This non-dom remittance basis is the central planning lever across all three scenarios.

### 1.1 Non-dom status

James was born in Australia and holds Australian domicile of origin. Moving to Malta for residency does not change his domicile unless he specifically acquires a Maltese domicile of choice (which he does not intend). Malta taxes a non-domiciliary resident on:

- **Malta-source income:** fully taxable at Malta personal rates.
- **Foreign-source income remitted to Malta:** taxable at Malta personal rates in the year of remittance.
- **Foreign-source income not remitted:** untaxed in Malta (subject to the minimum tax below).

### 1.2 Malta personal income tax — married couple rates (2026 reference)

| Taxable income (€) | Rate |
|---|---|
| 0 – 12,700 | 0% |
| 12,701 – 21,200 | 15% |
| 21,201 – 28,700 | 25% |
| 28,701 – 60,000 | 25% |
| Above 60,000 | 35% |

*Re-verify: Malta personal tax bands are adjusted by annual budget; confirm current 2026/27 rates with a Maltese advisor.*

### 1.3 Minimum tax (non-dom)

Where James's foreign-source income exceeds €35,000 in a tax year and he does not bring it all to Malta, a minimum Malta tax of approximately **€5,000 per married couple** applies. This is a floor — actual Malta tax liability applies if it exceeds the minimum.

### 1.4 Malta capital gains

Malta does not generally tax capital gains on **foreign assets** in the hands of a non-dom. Capital gains on Maltese immovable property are taxed separately (typically 8–10% final withholding on the transfer value). There is no annual CGT on shares or other movable property sold outside Malta.

### 1.5 What this means across the three scenarios

Every scenario below interacts with this baseline. The effective total rate depends on: corporate tax in the company's jurisdiction + any withholding tax on dividends + Malta personal tax on remitted amounts. The non-dom remittance basis can shelter foreign-company income that is not brought to Malta — but it cannot reduce corporate tax already paid in the company's country.

## 2. Scenario A — Australian Company, James Lives in Malta

> **TLDR:** The most expensive option. Australia taxes the company's worldwide income at 25-30% regardless of where James lives. Running the company from Malta risks also creating a Maltese permanent establishment or dual corporate residence on top of the Australian tax. Dividends to James as a non-Australian-resident attract 15% withholding under the Australia–Malta DTA. The non-dom remittance basis helps James personally but cannot reduce the underlying Australian corporate tax. If James still holds an Australian company at the point of move, the minimum action is a joint AU/MT specialist review before departure.

### 2.1 Australian corporate tax

An **Australian-incorporated company is an Australian tax resident by incorporation** — this does not change because James moves to Malta. Australia taxes the company on its worldwide income.

| Factor | Position |
|---|---|
| Corporate tax rate — base rate entity (turnover <A$50M, passive income ≤80%) | **25%** |
| Corporate tax rate — all other companies | **30%** |
| Taxed on | Worldwide income |
| Franking credits | Available for Australian-resident shareholders only; as a non-Australian-resident, James cannot use franking credits to offset Australian personal tax on dividends received |

### 2.2 Maltese exposure — dual-residence and PE risk

**The hard fact:** running an Australian company from Malta day-to-day typically creates exposure in **both** countries.

- **Dual corporate residence.** Malta taxes any company whose management and control is exercised in Malta as a Maltese-resident company (on a non-dom basis: Malta-source income + remitted foreign income). If James makes all management decisions from his Maltese home, Malta can claim the Australian company is also Maltese-resident.
- **Permanent establishment (PE).** Even without dual-residence, carrying on business in Malta through James as an agent who has authority to bind the company can create a Maltese PE, and Malta can tax the profits attributable to that PE.
- **Australia–Malta DTA (1984) framework.** Business profits are taxed in the company's country of residence unless attributable to a PE in the other country. For dual-resident companies, the treaty does not provide an automatic tie-breaker — both tax authorities must agree through a mutual agreement procedure (MAP), which is slow and uncertain.

*Practical implication:* "doing nothing" when James moves is the **highest-risk path**. It does not reduce Australian tax; it adds Maltese exposure on top.

### 2.3 Extracting money from the Australian company

Once the company has paid Australian corporate tax, James can receive money as:

**a) Salary / director's fees**
- The company deducts salary → reduces Australian corporate tax (saves 25–30 cents per dollar).
- Services rendered in Malta are **not** Australian-source income — they are Malta-source income.
- Malta taxes Malta-source income in full at progressive personal rates (up to 35%).
- Net effect: the corporate tax saving (~25-30%) is offset by Malta personal tax (up to 35%). Not a win.

**b) Dividends**
- Company first pays Australian corporate tax (25-30%).
- Dividends paid to James as a non-Australian-resident: **15% withholding tax** applies under the AU–Malta DTA (cap; confirm reduced rate for qualifying holdings with AU specialists).
- In Malta, dividends from an Australian company are **foreign-source income** for a non-dom James:
  - **Not remitted to Malta** → untaxed in Malta; minimum tax €5,000/yr applies.
  - **Remitted to Malta** → taxable at Malta personal rates (up to 35%), with credit for Australian withholding paid.

**c) Effective rate — Australian company**

| Extraction method | AU corp tax | DTA WHT | Malta personal | Approx. total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Salary (Malta-source services) | Zero (deducted) | — | Up to 35% | ~35% |
| Dividends — not remitted to Malta | 25-30% | 15% on net dividend | Zero | ~36-38% of gross profit |
| Dividends — remitted to Malta | 25-30% | 15% (credited in Malta) | Up to 35% (less credit) | ~35-36% |
| Profits retained in company indefinitely | 25-30% | — | — | ~25-30% (lowest, but locked in) |

*The "not remitted" line looks like 25-30% at the company level, but the 15% DTA withholding is also deducted from the dividend before it reaches James — effectively a further ~11-12% cost on the after-tax profit. Total on gross profit extracted: ~36-38%.*

### 2.4 Anti-avoidance traps

| Rule | Risk for James |
|---|---|
| **Personal Services Income (PSI)** | If the company's income is substantially James's own services (one main client, no employees, no significant capital employed), the ATO can attribute that income directly to James as though no company existed — losing the 25% corporate rate entirely. The test is fact-specific; verify with an AU specialist whether a tech-subscription business clears the bar. |
| **Controlled Foreign Company (CFC)** | Applies to Australian tax residents who control foreign companies. Once James ceases to be an Australian tax resident, he is not subject to CFC rules personally. However, during the transition period (while still nominally Australian-resident), CFC risks apply to any offshore entity he sets up ahead of the move. |
| **Transfer pricing** | Any charges between an Australian company and a related Maltese entity must be at arm's length. |

### 2.5 Australian exit CGT — CGT Event I1

When James **ceases to be an Australian tax resident**, CGT Event I1 triggers a deemed disposal at market value of his non-Taxable Australian Property (non-TAP) assets.

- Shares in a software/SaaS company (asset base predominantly intangibles) are typically **non-TAP** → deemed disposal on departure.
- **If the company is sold before James leaves Australia:** the sale is taxable at Australian CGT rates while James is still resident; the 50% CGT discount applies if shares held >12 months. No deemed disposal problem.
- **If the company still exists on departure:** deemed disposal at market value → Australian CGT on the unrealised gain (before the discount). An election to defer is available but is all-or-nothing across all foreign assets.

*For MoveData specifically:* The revised strategy memo (MAL-6) records that MoveData is being sold (gate O4). If sold before James departs, the exit-CGT issue on the MoveData shares disappears. The analysis above applies to any **new** Australian company James creates after the sale.

### 2.6 Verdict — Scenario A

**Not recommended as a Malta-optimised structure.** Australia's worldwide corporate tax (25-30%) cannot be reduced from Malta. The dual-residence and PE risk adds cost and regulatory complexity rather than reducing tax. Dividends to a non-resident are hit with 15% DTA withholding before reaching Malta. The non-dom remittance basis helps James personally on the dividend he receives, but the bulk of the economic cost is the corporate tax already paid in Australia.

**Minimum action before the move:** engage an Australian and Maltese tax advisor jointly to assess the dual-residence and PE position; address PSI risk; time any sale against departure to access the 50% CGT discount.

## 3. Scenario B — Singapore Company, James Lives in Malta

> **TLDR:** A Singapore company with genuine Singapore-resident management can achieve a low effective rate (~5-17% depending on scale) with no dividend withholding, and Malta's non-dom remittance basis means profits left in Singapore are not taxed in Malta (minimum €5,000/yr only). The critical catch: Singapore's tax residency test is management-and-control-based. If James runs the Singapore company entirely from Malta, it likely loses Singapore tax residency — and with it, every advantage. The structure requires a genuine Singapore-resident director and actual management activity in Singapore. It is a good fit if James has Singapore-based business reasons; it is overhead-heavy if the only motive is tax minimisation.

### 3.1 Singapore corporate tax

Singapore's territorial, one-tier system is well-suited to a non-dom Malta structure:

| Factor | Position |
|---|---|
| Standard corporate tax rate | **17%** |
| Dividend withholding tax | **Nil** — Singapore uses a one-tier system; dividends are exempt in the shareholder's hands |
| Capital gains tax | **Nil** |
| Territorial taxation | **Foreign-sourced income** is generally exempt from Singapore tax if received in Singapore from a jurisdiction with a headline rate ≥15% (or under specific exemptions). Singapore-source income is taxable at 17%. |

### 3.2 Singapore start-up and partial exemptions

**Start-Up Tax Exemption (SUTE) — first three qualifying years:**
- 75% exempt on the first SGD 100,000 of chargeable income → effective **4.25%** on that tranche.
- 50% exempt on the next SGD 100,000 → effective **8.5%** on that tranche.

**Partial Tax Exemption (PTE) — ongoing (after year 3 or for established companies):**
- 75% exempt on the first SGD 10,000 → effective 4.25%.
- 50% exempt on the next SGD 190,000 → effective 8.5%.
- Above SGD 200,000: flat 17%.

*Illustrative effective rates at different scales:*

| Annual chargeable income (SGD) | Approx. effective rate (SUTE) | Approx. effective rate (PTE, year 4+) |
|---|---|---|
| 100,000 | ~4.25% | ~5.5% |
| 200,000 | ~6.4% | ~9.1% |
| 500,000 | ~11.5% | ~14.5% |
| 930,000 (~A$1M ARR equivalent) | ~14.7% | ~16.1% |

*The exemptions are most valuable at early-stage or modest-revenue scale. As revenue scales, the effective rate converges toward 17%.*

### 3.3 The critical residency requirement — management and control

**Singapore's corporate tax residency test is management-and-control-based, not incorporation-based.** A Singapore-incorporated company whose management and control is exercised entirely outside Singapore is **not a Singapore tax resident**, and loses access to Singapore's tax treaties, territorial exemptions, and SUTE/PTE.

For James operating from Malta:
- If James is the **sole director making all decisions from Malta** → the company is likely **not Singapore-resident** → it may become a Maltese tax resident (managed and controlled in Malta) or a stateless entity. The Singapore structure then fails entirely.
- **Minimum requirement:** at least one **Singapore-resident director** who holds genuine board authority and makes substantive management decisions in Singapore. Annual general meetings and key board resolutions should occur in Singapore.

**Annual cost of Singapore substance (reference estimates, SGD):**

| Item | SGD/year |
|---|---|
| Professional / nominee director | 5,000 – 15,000 |
| Registered office + corporate secretary (required by law) | 1,000 – 3,000 |
| Annual compliance, accounting, tax filing | 5,000 – 15,000 |
| Audit (if revenue thresholds exceeded) | 5,000 – 20,000+ |

A Singapore company without genuine management substance does not pass scrutiny — Singapore's Inland Revenue Authority (IRAS) has tightened its position on companies claiming tax residency through nominee arrangements with no real activity.

### 3.4 Malta exposure — PE risk and personal tax

**PE risk in Malta:** James working from his Maltese home as a de-facto agent of the Singapore company who has authority to conclude contracts can create a **Maltese PE**. Business profits attributable to that PE are taxable in Malta (at 35% corporate rate, with the 6/7ths refund potentially available on Maltese-PE profits, subject to Malta Revenue's position).

*Practical mitigation:* If the Singapore company has a genuine Singapore director managing business decisions, and James's Malta role is clearly defined as technical/operational (not contract-binding), the PE risk can be managed — but not eliminated without specialist advice.

**Singapore–Malta DTA:** Verify with specialist counsel that a double tax agreement between Singapore and Malta is in force and covers the specific PE and residency scenarios described here. *[Flag: the existence and scope of this treaty must be confirmed by a Maltese and Singapore tax advisor before relying on it.]*

**James's personal Malta tax:**
- Singapore company dividends are **foreign-source income** for a Maltese non-dom James.
- **Not remitted to Malta** → untaxed in Malta; minimum tax €5,000/yr applies.
- **Remitted to Malta** → taxable at Malta personal rates (up to 35%); ordinary credit relief under any applicable DTA.

**Non-remittance strategy:** Leave profits in the Singapore company; James draws only what he needs to fund Malta living expenses. Malta personal tax = €5,000/yr minimum. Singapore corporate tax = ~5-17% depending on scale. This is a legitimate and clean structure if Singapore substance is maintained.

### 3.5 Effective rate — Singapore company

| Profit destination | Singapore corp tax | WHT on dividend | Malta personal | Malta minimum | Total effective |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Retained in Singapore (not remitted) | ~5-17% (scale-dep.) | Nil | Zero | €5,000/yr | **~5-17% + €5k/yr** |
| Remitted to Malta | ~5-17% | Nil | Up to 35% (less DTA credit) | — | ~35% (credit-limited) |
| Start-up scale (<SGD 200k chargeable) — not remitted | ~4-8.5% | Nil | Zero | €5,000/yr | **~4-8.5% + €5k/yr** |

### 3.6 Verdict — Scenario B

**Can be highly efficient — but is not a "set and forget" structure.** The non-remittance approach (profits accumulate in Singapore; James pays €5,000 Malta minimum) achieves very low all-in rates, particularly at modest scales. The absence of Singapore dividend withholding makes it cleaner than the Australian scenario.

**Works best when:**
- James has genuine Singapore business reasons (Asia-Pacific operations, Singapore customers or investors).
- He can establish and maintain real Singapore substance (local director, at minimum).
- He does not need to regularly draw down profits to Malta (or can fund Malta life from other sources).

**Not recommended** as the primary structure for someone with no pre-existing Singapore nexus who is simply seeking the lowest rate — the substance overhead makes it a more complex version of the Maltese company, which achieves ~5% on its own terms.

## 4. Scenario C — Maltese Company, James Lives in Malta

> **TLDR:** The cleanest structure for a Malta-based operator. The Maltese company pays 35% corporate tax, then shareholders claim a 6/7ths refund, bringing the effective rate to ~5% on trading income. No PE or dual-residence complexity — the company and James are in the same place. Full substance by default. The 35% headline rate is not the real rate; the refund mechanism makes Malta genuinely competitive. This is the recommended structure if James is building and running a business from Malta.

### 4.1 Malta corporate tax and the 6/7ths refund

Malta uses a **full imputation system** with a corporate tax rate of 35%. The refund mechanism makes the real rate far lower:

**How the 6/7ths refund works (€100 example):**
1. Maltese company earns **€100** of trading profit.
2. Company pays **€35** corporate tax → retains **€65**.
3. Company declares a **€65 dividend** to James.
4. James (as shareholder) claims a **6/7ths refund** from Malta Revenue: 6/7 × €35 = **€30 refund** paid out in cash.
5. **Net tax on €100** = €35 − €30 = **€5 → effective rate: 5%.**

| Income type | Refund fraction | Corporate tax paid | Refund received | **Effective rate** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Trading income (active) | **6/7ths** | 35% | 30% | **~5%** |
| Passive income (interest, royalties) | **5/7ths** | 35% | 25% | **~10%** |
| Dividends received by Maltese company | **2/3rds** | 35% | ~23.3% | **~11.7%** |

*The 6/7ths refund applies to income allocated to the Malta Tax Account (MTA). Verify which account applies to the company's specific income streams with a Maltese advisor.*

### 4.2 Timing of the refund

The 6/7ths refund is paid by Malta Revenue **after** the shareholder claims it — typically **6–14 weeks** after the dividend is declared and the company's tax settled. The company pays 35% first; the refund follows. This cash-flow lag must be planned for: the company should retain a working capital buffer equal to at least the corporate tax liability before declaring dividends.

### 4.3 James's personal Malta tax — full imputation eliminates double tax

James receives a dividend from his Maltese company. Maltese company dividends are **Malta-source income** — the non-dom remittance basis does not apply to shelter them. However, the full imputation system eliminates double taxation at the personal level:

- The dividend carries an **imputation credit** equal to the corporate tax paid (35% of pre-tax profit).
- James's personal tax is assessed on the **grossed-up dividend** (the pre-tax profit figure).
- The imputation credit offsets the personal tax liability.
- At the top personal rate of 35%, the imputation credit fully offsets the personal tax → **zero additional personal tax** on the dividend.
- James then separately receives the **€30 refund** (6/7ths) in cash from Malta Revenue.

**Net result:** James pays approximately **5% total** on the company's trading profits, all in Malta, via the corporate tax net of the refund. No additional layer of personal tax because the imputation eliminates it.

### 4.4 Substance requirements

A Maltese company needs genuine economic substance in Malta:
- Registered office in Malta.
- Director(s) resident and active in Malta (James satisfies this inherently).
- Annual accounts filed; audit required above statutory thresholds.
- VAT registration if supplying goods/services to EU customers above the registration threshold.

This is James's key advantage: he is building his business in Malta, so substance is satisfied by default. There is no need to create substance in a third country through nominees.

### 4.5 VAT / EU considerations

Because Malta is an EU member state, a Maltese company trading within the EU must:
- Register for Maltese VAT (standard rate: 18%).
- For B2B EU supplies: reverse charge applies (no Maltese VAT obligation on the supply itself).
- For B2C EU supplies: One-Stop-Shop (OSS) registration allows single-country filing.
- For non-EU customers: no EU VAT obligation.

VAT is a cash-flow and compliance matter, not a net cost — VAT collected from customers is remitted to Malta Revenue. However it adds filing obligations and must be built into invoicing and pricing from day one.

### 4.6 No PE or dual-residence problem

Both James and his company are in Malta → **no foreign PE exposure and no dual-residence conflict**. The company is Maltese-resident; its income is taxed in Malta. The only treaty interactions arise if the company derives income from foreign sources (e.g., customers in Australia pay royalties to the Maltese company → potential AU withholding tax capped by the AU–Malta DTA at 10% for royalties).

### 4.7 Effective rate — Maltese company

| Income type | Corp tax (gross) | 6/7ths refund | Personal imputation | **Net effective rate** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Trading profit (active business) | 35% | −30% (refund to shareholder) | Fully offset by imputation credit | **~5%** |
| Passive income (interest, royalties) | 35% | −25% (5/7ths refund) | Fully offset | **~10%** |

### 4.8 Holding company layer (optional)

For additional flexibility — particularly if James has co-investors, wants to ring-fence assets, or holds the Maltese company through a family trust — a foreign **holding company** (e.g., a BVI, Cyprus, or Dutch holding company) can sit above the Maltese operating company and claim the 6/7ths refund as the shareholder. Dividends flow from the Maltese operating company → holding company (which claims the refund) → James personally. This adds a layer of complexity and compliance cost; it is only warranted if there is a specific structural reason.

### 4.9 Verdict — Scenario C

**Recommended as the primary structure for a Malta-based operator.** The ~5% effective rate on trading income is competitive with any leading jurisdiction. The structure is clean, well-understood, internationally respected (Malta is an EU member and OECD-compliant), and eliminates PE and dual-residence risk. Compliance is concentrated in one jurisdiction. Set-up is straightforward: a Maltese Private Limited Company (Ltd) typically incorporates in 2–4 weeks.

**Key planning points:**
- **Dividend timing:** Declare dividends only when the company has settled its corporate tax — the refund arrives 6–14 weeks later. Maintain a tax-payment buffer.
- **Salary vs. dividend:** Salary paid to James is deductible (reducing corporate tax base) but is Malta-source income taxable at personal rates (up to 35%). For most owner-operators, **a modest salary + the bulk as dividends** (claiming the refund) achieves the lowest blended rate. A Maltese tax advisor should model the optimal split.
- **Income classification:** Ensure trading income is clearly distinguished from passive income (10% effective rate) in the company's accounts.

## 7. Key Caveats and Professional Sign-Off Required
