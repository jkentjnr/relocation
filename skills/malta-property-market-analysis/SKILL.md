# Malta Property Market Analysis

Use this skill when you need to ground a property decision in the current state of the Malta market rather than assumptions.

## When to use
- At the start of a market-research workstream, to set a price baseline.
- Whenever you need to know "what does EUR X actually buy, and where?"

## Method
1. **Define the budget envelope.** Start from the client's budget ceiling (from the engagement brief) and immediately distinguish *purchase price* from *all-in cost* — acquisition costs add roughly 6–9% (stamp duty, notary, searches, agency). The effective property ceiling is below the cash ceiling.
2. **Pull current price levels** for the relevant property types (apartment, maisonette, terraced/townhouse, finished vs shell) and express them as price per m² by region. Re-verify against live sources; do not rely on memory.
3. **Read the trend.** Note recent annual price growth, the interest-rate environment, new-build supply, and the foreign-demand drivers (residency programmes, remote workers). Keep macro context in service of the client's decision.
4. **Map budget to options.** Produce a short table: for the client's all-in budget, what type/size/finish is realistic in each candidate region.
5. **Flag distortions.** New-build vs resale, asking vs achieved price, and shell-and-core vs turnkey can swing the real cost materially.

## 2026 reference points (re-verify at runtime)
- Acquisition costs are non-trivial: budget ~5% stamp duty (relief can reduce this on a residence), notary ~1–2.5%, plus searches and agency.
- Harbour/urban-core stock (Sliema, St Julian's, Gzira) carries the highest €/m²; the north and Gozo are materially cheaper per m².

## Output
A market baseline: a price-per-m² table by region and property type, a "what the budget buys" summary, the trend read, and an explicit note of the all-in vs headline cost gap.

## Sources
Use the agency, portal, and official-data list in `references/malta-property-sources.md` to pull live listings, asking/achieved prices, and statistics. Cross-check at least two sources.

## Caveats
Figures move quickly. Treat the reference points as a sanity check, not a source — always cite live data in the deliverable.
